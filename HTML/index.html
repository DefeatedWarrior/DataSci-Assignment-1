Understanding Social Media Use in Teenagers

Introduction

Social media plays a major role in the daily lives of teenagers, providing entertainment, connection, and a sense of belonging. For many young people like Liam, a 15-year-old student, social media is a normal part of everyday life. However, with Australia introducing new laws restricting access to social media for users under 16, questions arise about whether this level of usage is harmless or potentially harmful. This data story explores how social media is used, what drives that behaviour, and whether the patterns observed support the need for such restrictions.

Behaviour: Notifications and Usage

An analysis of Liam’s phone data reveals a strong relationship between the number of notifications received and how frequently apps are opened. Apps that generate higher numbers of notifications are opened significantly more often, and are also associated with longer total usage time. This suggests that user behaviour is not entirely intentional, but is influenced by external prompts designed to capture attention. Rather than choosing to engage with apps, users may be reacting to constant notifications, leading to repeated and often unconscious checking. This highlights the role of app design in shaping behaviour, raising concerns about how much control users truly have over their own screen time.

Attention: Which Apps Dominate

Further analysis shows that a small number of apps account for the majority of total screen time. These apps, primarily social media and entertainment platforms, dominate Liam’s attention while other apps are used far less frequently. This uneven distribution suggests that certain platforms are significantly more effective at farming user engagement. Features such as infinite scrolling, personalised content, and algorithm-driven recommendations likely contribute to this dominance. As a result, user attention is concentrated on a limited number of platforms that are specifically designed to maximise time spent, reinforcing patterns of prolonged use.

Comparison: Liam vs Other Students

When Liam’s usage is compared to a broader dataset of student social media use, it becomes clear that his behaviour reflects a wider trend among teenagers. Whether his usage is slightly above or close to the average, the data indicates that high levels of screen time are common. This suggests that the issue is not limited to one individual, but is instead a widespread pattern across young people. The normalisation of high social media usage may make it more difficult for individuals to recognise potential risks, as their behaviour appears typical within their peer group.

Impact: Mental Health and Sleep

Data from a larger student dataset also reveals a relationship between increased social media usage and negative outcomes such as lower mental health scores and reduced sleep duration. As daily usage increases, there is a general trend toward poorer wellbeing indicators. While this relationship does not prove that social media directly causes these issues, it does suggest a meaningful association that cannot be ignored. These findings support concerns that excessive engagement with social media may have broader impacts beyond simple time use, affecting both physical and mental wellbeing.

Trends, Variability, Bias and Ethics

Across the datasets, several clear trends emerge. Higher notification levels are linked to increased app engagement, and greater overall usage is associated with potential negative impacts on wellbeing. However, there is also variability in the data, with usage patterns differing between apps and across different days, suggesting that behaviour is influenced by multiple factors such as time, context, and individual habits. It is important to acknowledge the limitations of this analysis, as the self-collected data represents only one individual over a short time period, which may not fully reflect broader populations. Additionally, there are ethical considerations surrounding both the design of social media platforms and the introduction of government restrictions. While platforms may use features that encourage addictive behaviour, policies that limit access must balance protecting young users with preserving their autonomy and social connection.

Conclusion

Overall, the data suggests that social media usage is not solely a matter of personal choice, but is strongly influenced by the design of the platforms themselves. Notifications, engagement features, and algorithm-driven content all contribute to increased usage and repeated interaction. When combined with evidence of potential negative impacts on wellbeing, these findings provide a strong justification for the introduction of restrictions on younger users. While such laws may appear strict, they aim to address a system that is designed to maximise engagement rather than support healthy usage. Understanding these patterns allows users like Liam to make more informed decisions about their behaviour, and highlights the importance of balancing connection with control.
